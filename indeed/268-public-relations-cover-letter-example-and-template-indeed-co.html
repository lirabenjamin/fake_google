Public Relations Cover Letter Example and Template

Written by

Updated June 6, 2025

Public relations, or PR, professionals create and maintain a positive public image of a company or organization through media outlets and personal appearances. job descriptions in this professional field include social media manager, publicist and PR manager. If you're interested in pursuing a career in public relations, learning how to craft a compelling cover letter can help you get a job in this field. In this article, we explain what to include in a cover letter for a public relations position and share an example of a public relations cover letter. Before creating your own public relations resume, you might review resume templates and examples to see some different formatting options. After you've decided on a format, you can upload a resume file or build an Indeed Resume to start your job hunt.

What to include in a public relations cover letter

While the exact contents of a public relations cover letter might depend on the job requirements and the candidate's qualifications, most cover letters for public relations positions contain the following information:

Contact information

Most cover letters begin with the candidate's contact information, which makes it easy for a recruiter or hiring manager to contact them and schedule an interview or phone screening. Provide your full name, along with the city and state where you live. A hiring manager might ask you for your full address at a later point in the application process. Also, include your phone number and a professional email address. If you have an online portfolio or website of your public relations work, you can add the URL for this resource under your email address.

A summary of your qualifications and goals

Beginning the body of your cover letter with a statement of your key skills and professional goals can encourage the hiring manager to continue reading your cover letter. You might include information about your experience in the field, like the number of years you've worked in public relations or the industries you've supported in your work. If you have PR certifications, you might mention them in this statement to verify your skills in the field. Finally, you can describe your professional goals, like working for an enterprise-level company, to show the hiring manager your potential for advancement and leadership.Related: Public Relations: Everything You Need To Know About Working in PR

A description of your professional experience

Your cover letter is an opportunity to describe your professional experience with more detail than your resume might allow. Select one or two positions that taught you valuable knowledge about PR to include in your cover letter. When you describe these jobs, include information about your title and the industry you worked in, along with key responsibilities. After you describe the job requirements, add details about your successes in the job and the impact your work had on the company. If you're beginning your PR career, you can describe projects from your degree program or any internships you completed.Related: How to Write a Cover Letter With No Experience: Your Entry-Level Cover Letter

Details about your skills

After you describe your professional experience, you can provide more details about the skills and certifications you mention at the beginning of your letter. Public relations professionals use a wide range of professional abilities, including organization and both verbal and written communication skills. You can review the job posting to identify the key skills expected for the position and include information about your abilities in these areas. For example, if the job posting mentions social media management, you can discuss your skills using a variety of platforms. You can also include details about your software proficiency and language skills.Related: Essential Public Relations Skills and How To Improve Them

A call to action

A call to action is a statement that invites the reader to perform a specific task, like scheduling an interview or visiting the candidate's online portfolio. Ending your cover letter with a call to action can show the hiring manager that you're enthusiastic about the job opportunity, which might give you an advantage in the hiring process. You might describe your contact availability or suggest the hiring manager look at your attached work samples. After making your call to action, thank the hiring manager for their time and consideration.Related: How To Write a Call-To-Action in Your Cover Letter
